# Communities, Source Records & Digitized Collections

When self-serve searching of engines and archives is exhausted, the next layer is the body of knowledge people and institutions have already published online: community catalogs and threads, scanned newspapers, library catalogs, and digitized physical-media collections. Mine these as searchable sources too — they hold both finished answers (already-found media, confirmed details) and the obscure links shared within them.

## 1. Community knowledge to mine

- **The Lost Media Wiki (lostmediawiki.com)** — the central hub: cataloged entries by status (lost / partially lost / found / existence unconfirmed), plus active **forum** threads and a **Discord** of collaborative hunts. Search existing entries and threads first — they record what's already been tried and often link the obscure sources that earlier hunters dug up.
- **r/lostmedia and tip-of-my-tongue subreddits** — searchable archives of past hunts; the same item may already have a solved or in-progress thread with leads.
- **WatZatSong** — for any music/lostwave identification (see identifying-unknown-media.md), both as a database of solved IDs and as a live ID source.
- **Topic-specific communities** — fandom wikis/Discords/subreddits and preservation groups for the show, game, studio, era, or country, plus the origin country's own forums and platforms (with translated searches) for foreign media. These specialists' pages frequently document exactly where a niche item lives.

## 2. Source records & credits

The original production usually left an online paper trail that points straight to surviving material. Mine it:
- **Credits and databases** — IMDb, IMFDB, AnimeNewsNetwork, MobyGames, Discogs, 45cat, and similar fan databases pin down exact titles, creators, air/release dates, and catalog numbers that re-feed every other search.
- **Studio / network / distributor sites** — official and archived versions often list catalogs, press releases, and release histories; their old Wayback captures can reveal items pulled from the live site.
- **Music cue sheets and licensing records** — for a song used in a show/ad, published cue sheets, ASCAP/BMI repertory databases, and soundtrack listings frequently name the exact track.
- **Uploader trails** — for a deleted video, the original channel's other uploads, archived metadata, and reupload chains often lead to a surviving copy.

## 3. Libraries & catalogs (WorldCat)

- **WorldCat (worldcat.org)** — indexes the holdings of libraries and archives worldwide; the best tool for locating a physical copy (a film print, script, recording, or book) that exists offline. Use its **advanced search** to cut through noise — it can surface, e.g., a pilot episode or scripts held by a specific library.
- **National/film archives & university special collections** — for broadcast and film material; many have searchable catalogs and will respond to research requests.
- **Open Library / Google Books / Amazon "look inside"** — for print and for references that confirm existence, credits, and dates.

## 4. Newspaper & print archives

Old print is a powerful way to **confirm a work existed**, fix its exact title/date, and find contemporaneous detail (TV listings, reviews, ads, production notes):
- **Newspapers.com** (subscription), and **free** options: the **California Digital Newspaper Collection (cdnc.ucr.edu)** and **Fulton History (fultonhistory.com)**, plus national library newspaper portals.
- TV-listing archives and trade publications (variety/broadcasting trades) pin down air dates and episode titles that re-feed the main search.

## 5. Marketplaces & digitized physical media

Genuinely un-digitized media often survives on tapes and discs, and traces of it surface online — search these directly:
- **eBay and marketplaces** — search **completed/sold listings** and seller photos, which frequently document a rare release's existence, catalog number, and packaging even after the auction ends. Also Craigslist, Mercari, Yahoo Auctions (Japan), Discogs marketplace, and regional equivalents.
- **Collector & digitizer uploads** — people who own old VHS/Betamax/cassettes/floppies/discs routinely digitize and post them to YouTube, Internet Archive, and preservation sites; search by exact title, catalog number, and origin language.
- **Location/landmark tracing** — when a hunt needs to pin down a building or place (e.g. from an address on a tape), Google Maps Street View's **time-machine** (historical imagery) and Wikipedia's GeoHack coordinates can locate older sites; useful for verifying a production company or filming location.

When a copy is located, the hunt isn't done until it's **digitized and shared/archived** (e.g. uploaded to Internet Archive) — preservation is the actual goal.
